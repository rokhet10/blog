There's a category of thing that's worth building even if no one ever uses it.

You learn things by building that you can't learn any other way — about the problem, about the tools, about your own judgment. The artifact is almost secondary. The process is the point.

## The mistake people make

A lot of people wait until they have the "right" idea before building anything. They want the thing to be worth something before they invest the time.

This gets it backwards. You don't know if an idea is worth anything until you've built enough of it to see what it actually is. The idea in your head and the thing you end up making are rarely the same.

## Starting small is not the same as thinking small

Starting small is a constraint on scope, not on ambition. It means: what's the smallest version of this that would tell me something real?

A working prototype of the wrong thing is more useful than a complete spec for the right thing, because it generates new information. You can react to it. You can show it to people and watch how they respond. You can see where your assumptions were wrong.

## What I'm trying to build toward

I don't have a clean answer here. I'm more interested in the shape of the question than any particular answer — what makes something worth the effort, independent of whether it succeeds?

I think it's whether you'd want the thing to exist in the world even if you couldn't take credit for it. That's a decent filter.

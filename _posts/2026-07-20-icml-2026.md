I spent the last week at ICML, meeting exciting new people and learning about their research. Although my favorite part was visiting the company booths and collecting free swag, it was also fascinating to attend keynote talks, explore posters, and ask researchers about the problems they are working on.<br>

One speaker who really stood out to me was Milind Tambe, a computer science professor at Harvard. His work focuses on AI for Social Good (AI4Good), including using artificial intelligence and game theory to help prevent terrorist attacks and protect vulnerable public spaces. I had never really thought about AI being applied to problems like this, and it was exciting to see how computer science can have such a direct impact on real-world safety.<br>

Along with all of these amazing experiences, I also had the opportunity to present my own research.<br>

Our paper introduces PoliticsBench, a benchmark for evaluating political bias in large language models (LLMs). While many existing benchmarks attempt to measure political bias, we found that they have two major limitations.<br>

First, they rely on static prompts. They often ask simple yes/no questions or direct questions such as, "What are your views on increasing the minimum wage to $20?"<br>

Why this is a problem: Modern LLMs are trained to remain neutral when asked explicit political questions. Instead of revealing how they actually reason, they often respond with something like, "As an AI, I don't have political opinions," or present both sides equally. This produces safe but uninformative answers that don't reveal the values guiding the model's decisions.<br>

Second, they usually score responses on a single political axis, such as left vs. right or authoritarian vs. libertarian.<br>

Why this is a problem: A one-dimensional score tells you where a model falls, but not why. Two models could receive the same political score while arriving there for completely different reasons and value systems.<br>

To address these issues, we created multi-stage role-play scenarios. Instead of answering a single question, models experience evolving situations where they must make difficult tradeoffs, justify their decisions, and sometimes sacrifice one value for another. This encourages deeper reasoning and produces much richer responses than traditional benchmarks.<br>

Rather than asking judge models to simply assign a political label, we ask them to score responses across ten underlying value dimensions that together provide a much more detailed picture of a model's political reasoning. Across twenty scenarios and eight leading LLMs, we found that these interactive role-play settings revealed significantly stronger and more diverse value expression than standard direct questioning.<br>

Presenting this work at ICML was an incredible experience. It was exciting to discuss the project with researchers from around the world, answer questions about our methodology, and hear ideas for where this type of research could go next. It made me even more excited to continue exploring AI research in the future.

I used to think learning fast was the goal. Get through the textbook, finish the course, check the box. There's a version of "fast" that actually works — but it's not the one I was doing.

The kind of fast I was doing was motion without traction. Pages turned, videos watched, problem sets submitted. But very little of it stuck in a way that transferred anywhere useful.

## The compounding problem

Understanding compounds. A concept you genuinely grasp becomes a hook that new ideas attach to. A concept you've only surface-learned sits there, isolated, and doesn't help you with anything adjacent.

So if you move through ten topics slowly enough to understand them, you're actually better positioned to learn the eleventh than someone who skimmed all twenty. The slow path catches up.

## What slow actually looks like

It doesn't mean reading every word carefully or never skipping ahead. It means:

- Stopping when something doesn't make sense, instead of continuing and hoping it resolves
- Working examples by hand before looking at solutions
- Explaining the idea to yourself out loud (or in writing) and noticing where you stumble
- Revisiting things after a few days to see what actually stayed

The last one is underrated. You find out very quickly what you understood versus what you recognized.

## The uncomfortable part

Slow learning is uncomfortable because progress feels invisible. Fast learning at least gives you the sensation of moving. There's a reason people prefer it even when it doesn't work.

The only thing that's helped me is trusting the evidence: when I've actually learned something slowly and well, it pays off visibly later. That's enough to keep going.
